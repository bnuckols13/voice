A Reminder Before We Begin

There is no right way to do this work. The goal is not a beautiful creation — the goal is an honest one. You may not finish a station. You may sit in stillness for a full round. You may make something private that only you see. All of this is exactly right.

In this group, silence is never empty. It is one of the most powerful forms of expression we have.

"Your silence will not protect you. But for every real word spoken, for every attempt I had ever made to speak those truths for which I am still seeking, I had made contact with other women while we examined the words to fit a world in which we all believed."
— Audre Lorde, The Cancer Journals

Opening Meditation

Find a comfortable position and allow your eyes to soften or close.

Begin by noticing the sounds in this room — let them wash over you without naming them. Just receive them.

Now draw your attention inward. Notice the sound of your own breath. The small creak of your exhale. The quiet between inhale and exhale — that pause that belongs to no one, that exists in pure stillness.

Now I want you to notice: somewhere in your body, there is a voice. Not necessarily the one you use to speak — but the one that knows things. The one that has things to say that you haven't found words for yet.

Today, we create space for that voice. We also honor the silence it sometimes chooses.

Place one hand on your throat, lightly. Notice what it feels like to hold this place of sound-making.

Take one breath in. Let it out slowly, as sound — even a whisper, even a sigh. Let that be enough.

Open your eyes when you're ready.

Therapeutic Frame

Why Voice & Silence?

Voice is not only what we say aloud. It is how we claim our existence in relationship — to others, to ourselves. Silence is not only absence. It can be a refuge, a protest, a form of protection, a place of deep knowing.

In recovery, in healing, in finding our way back to ourselves, we encounter both: the words we've been waiting to speak, and the silences that have kept us safe (or kept us small). Today we explore both without judgment — honoring the wisdom in each.

Understanding Voice & Silence

Tap any card to explore the science and wisdom behind today's theme.

〰
The Vocal Nervous System
▾

Your voice is a direct expression of your nervous system state. The social engagement system — the ventral vagal branch — actually innervates the muscles of your face, throat, and ears. When you feel safe, your voice carries warmth, rhythm, and melody. When your system detects danger, your vocal prosody flattens, speeds up, or disappears entirely.

This means that the quality of your voice — not just the words — communicates safety or threat to everyone who hears you. And when you hear a warm, rhythmic voice, your own nervous system co-regulates in response.

Notice: What does your voice sound like when you are most yourself? What happens to it when you feel unsafe, unheard, or unseen?
◯
The Many Kinds of Silence
▾

Not all silence is the same. Learning to distinguish between different silences can help us understand what our nervous systems and our inner worlds are doing.

  • Restorative silence — the peace of not needing to perform or explain. A pause in good company.
  • Protective silence — the quiet we retreat into when speech feels too dangerous. A part keeping us safe.
  • Suppressed silence — words swallowed. Things we know but have decided (or been taught) cannot be said.
  • Sacred silence — the deliberate, chosen stillness of contemplative practice. The space between notes.
  • Frozen silence — the dissociated quiet of overwhelm, when the nervous system goes offline.
Which of these silences do you know well? Which have you lived in most?
▽
What We Swallow
▾

Narrative therapy teaches us that our stories shape our identities — and that many of our most limiting stories were authored by others. We internalize the voices of people who told us we were too loud, too much, too sensitive, wrong to feel what we felt. Over time, those external voices can become an internal censor that silences us before we even begin.

The throat — symbolically and somatically — is the place where voice meets decision. Where "will I speak or not?" lives in the body. Many of us hold chronic tension there, a kind of permanent swallowing of the things we couldn't safely say.

What story lives in your throat? What part of you has been doing the swallowing — and what might it need?
❋
IFS: Parts That Speak & Parts That Silence
▾

In Internal Family Systems, we understand that different parts of us have very different relationships to voice. Some parts are outspoken — the ones that demand, protest, explain, or perform. Other parts are the silencers — protectors that learned it wasn't safe to speak, or that speech always led to hurt.

Neither is wrong. Both are trying to help the system survive.

The invitation is to get curious about which part is in charge of your voice in different moments. The part that performs confidence at work. The part that goes quiet in conflict. The part that whispers your deepest truths only to yourself.

Can you notice two different parts — one that wants to speak and one that wants to stay quiet? What does each one need right now?
◈
NVC: Voice as Needs in Motion
▾

Marshall Rosenberg taught that underneath every word — every criticism, every silence, every demand — is a need seeking to be met. When we speak reactively, we are often voicing a need in its most disguised form. When we go silent, we are often protecting a need that feels too vulnerable to name.

Finding our voice in NVC is not about speaking more — it's about speaking from the truth beneath the armor. Observation, feeling, need, request: a path from reactivity to genuine contact.

What need is waiting to be voiced in you today? Not the argument — the need underneath it.
☽
Noble Silence & Mindful Speech
▾

Buddhist psychology distinguishes between noble silence — the purposeful, spacious quiet that allows wisdom to arise — and the silence of avoidance or suppression. Noble silence is not emptiness. It is presence without the compulsive filling of space with words.

Right Speech, one of the eight limbs of the Noble Eightfold Path, asks us: Is it true? Is it kind? Is it helpful? Is it the right time? When all four conditions are met, speak. When they are not, noble silence may be the wiser choice.

When have you experienced noble silence — a quiet that felt full rather than empty? What made it different?
∿
Process Over Perfection
▾

Perfectionism is one of the most common ways we silence ourselves before we even begin. It says: only speak when you have it right. Only create when the result will be beautiful. Only share when you are certain. And so we wait — sometimes forever — for a readiness that perfectionism never allows.

Research on creativity and healing consistently shows that the process of making — not the product — is where transformation lives. The act of moving a brush, of writing a messy first sentence, of humming something with no melody — these are neurologically regulating. They shift our state. The finished piece is a byproduct of a process that was already doing its work.

  • The inner critic speaks in finished products. It evaluates outcomes. But the present moment of making has no outcome yet — only experience.
  • Mistakes are data, not verdicts. When your voice cracks, when the drawing looks "wrong," when the movement feels awkward — these are the moments most worth staying with.
  • The body doesn't care about beauty. It cares about expression. Release. Contact. Movement. The nervous system responds to the act of making regardless of what gets made.
Notice: Is there a part of you waiting until you can do this "right"? What would it mean to begin anyway — imperfectly, honestly, now?
⌁
Regulation & the Window of Tolerance
▾

Your nervous system exists on a spectrum. At the center is your window of tolerance — the zone where you can think, feel, and engage at the same time. This is where learning, connection, and creativity are most available. When we move outside this window — into overwhelm above, or shutdown below — expression becomes harder and protection takes over.

Both hyperarousal and hypoarousal are deeply connected to voice and silence:

  • Hyperarousal (above the window): Voice becomes reactive — loud, fast, pressured. Words spill before they're chosen. This is fight/flight speaking.
  • Hypoarousal (below the window): Voice disappears. Throat tightens. We freeze or collapse. This is shutdown silence — not chosen, but imposed by the nervous system for protection.
  • Within the window: Voice can be chosen. Silence can be chosen. Expression becomes genuine rather than reactive or shut-down.

Expressive arts work — movement, sound, image-making — is one of the most reliable ways to return to the window when we've drifted. The body finds regulation through doing, often before the mind catches up.

Right now, where do you sense yourself? In your window? Edging toward overwhelm? Tending toward quiet or flatness? There is no wrong answer — just useful information.
Hyperarousal — racing, flooding, reactive voice
Window of Tolerance — present, expressive, connected
Hypoarousal — numb, flat, voice retreats inward
Expressive Arts

Seven Stations

Move freely between stations. Stay as long as you need. Skip any that don't feel right. There is no order, no comparison, no finished product required. Let the materials meet you where you are.

Exploring what wants to be expressed today

Station I
Visual Arts
The Unsaid, Made Visible

Drawing, painting, collage, mark-making

  1. Draw or paint your voice — not what it says, but what it looks like. Shape, color, texture, weight.
  2. Create a map of your silences. Where do you go quiet? Where are you loudest? What does each territory look like?
  3. Make "The Unsaid Letter" — a visual piece representing something you have never been able to put into words. No words needed. Just image.
  4. Draw the throat as a landscape. What lives there? What is growing? What is held?
  5. Create a portrait of yourself with the voice you were born with — before you learned to modify it.
✦ This station is always optional. You may observe, sketch quietly, or simply be present here.
Station II
Movement & Dance
What the Body Says

Somatic movement, gesture, breath-led motion

  1. Move like a sound wave — let your whole body trace the shape of sound through air. Big waves. Tiny ripples. Stillness between.
  2. Explore "swallowing" as a physical act. What does your body do when you swallow a word? Where does it go? Move that story.
  3. Find the movement of your truest voice — not performed, not shaped for others. What does that look like in your body?
  4. Alternate between taking up space and collapsing. Notice when each feels like speaking, and when each feels like silence.
  5. Move in silence for one full minute. Then let sound enter the movement. What changes?
✦ Seated, standing, gentle stretching — all movement is valid here. Do only what is comfortable for your body.
Station III
Music & Sound
Breath Is the First Instrument

Humming, breath, percussion, ambient sound

  1. Hum something — not a song, just a sound that wants to come out. Let it be wordless. Let it change.
  2. Create a "sound portrait" of a silence you know well — using breath, rhythm, or the instruments here, make silence audible.
  3. Whisper something true. Then say it aloud. Then say it again louder. Notice what shifts in your body at each volume.
  4. Create a 13-beat rhythm that alternates between sound and silence. This is the rhythm of voice — the space between notes matters as much as the notes themselves.
  5. Sit in intentional silence for three minutes. What sounds do you notice? What arises in the absence of speech?
✦ You may use any instrument, your voice, or simply your breath at this station.
Station IV
Writing & Poetry
The Letter Never Sent

Journaling, poetry, lists, letters

  1. Write the letter you have never sent. You do not have to send it today. You do not even have to finish it. Just begin.
  2. Write a list: "Things I say." Then write: "Things I have never said." Let the second list be as long as it wants to be.
  3. Describe your silence in the third person — as if it were a character. Who is it? What does it protect? What does it need?
  4. Write about a time you spoke and wished you hadn't. Then write about a time you stayed silent and wished you hadn't. What do these stories share?
  5. Finish this sentence as many ways as you can: "What I have always wanted to say is…"
✦ Nothing written here needs to be shared. These words belong to you.
Station V
Drama & Performance
The Moment Before Speaking

Role play, embodiment, tableau, voice work

  1. Enact "the moment before speaking" — the pause where the decision is made. Make it long, visible, physical. What lives in that pause?
  2. Play a scene where two people communicate entirely without words. What gets through? What is lost?
  3. Give voice to the part of you that has been silent the longest. What does it want to say, now that it finally has the stage?
  4. Practice saying something difficult — something you've rehearsed internally. Say it to the room, or to an empty chair, or to a mirror. Notice the difference between the rehearsal and the saying.
  5. Create a brief tableau — a frozen moment — of speaking your truth. Hold it. Then let it thaw.
✦ You may work solo, in pairs, or as an observer at this station.
Station VI
Fashion & Adornment
Adorning the Place of Voice

Collage, wearable art, decoration, crafting

  1. Create an adornment for the throat — a collar, a necklace, a scarf, or simply a drawn piece worn at the neck. What do you want to protect there? What do you want to celebrate?
  2. Design something that represents your voice at its most free. What would you wear if you were speaking your fullest truth?
  3. Create a visual symbol for a silence you are ready to break — something to mark this moment of transition.
  4. Adorn yourself for the voice you are growing into — not the one you have always used, but the one that is becoming.
  5. Make a "talisman of speaking" — a small object that represents the courage it takes to be heard.
✦ Wearable or visual art both welcome here. Take your talisman with you if you wish.
Station VII
Open Intuitive
Follow What Arises

No prompts. No instructions. Just presence.

This station has no prompts. You may use any materials, any modality, or none at all.

You may sit in silence. You may use the materials in ways they weren't intended. You may work across multiple mediums at once.

The only question is: what wants to happen, if you stop directing and start listening?

✦ Sitting quietly or doing nothing is a full and complete use of this station.
Integration

Returning to Words

Take a few moments to settle. Breathe. Let whatever arose in the stations begin to land. These reflections are for you — private unless you choose to share.
